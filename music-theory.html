Quick tip

There are 12 pitches in an octave — 7 natural notes plus 5 sharps/flats. Every sharp has an enharmonic flat twin (same pitch, different name). The French system uses solfège syllables and is standard in France, Switzerland, Spain, and Italy. Si is sometimes written Ti in English-speaking countries.

Major modes — brighter, more resolved
Ionian (major)
Mode I · W W H W W W H
Bright, happy, resolved. The most "neutral" and optimistic sound in Western music.
joyful uplifting stable
Lydian
Mode IV · W W W H W W H
Dreamy, ethereal, floating. Major with a raised 4th — adds wonder and magic.

Mixolydian
Mode V · W W H W W H W
Bright but slightly unresolved. Major feel with a bluesy, rock edge from the flat 7th.

Minor modes — darker, more expressive
Aeolian (natural minor)
Mode VI · W H W W H W W
Sad, melancholic, introspective. The most common minor sound — heavy but familiar.
sad melancholic dark
Dorian
Mode II · W H W W W H W
Minor but with a hopeful quality. Sad yet sophisticated — jazz, soul, and folk love this one.
soulful bittersweet groovy
Phrygian
Mode III · H W W W H W W
Dark, tense, Spanish-flavored. The flat 2nd gives it an exotic, almost menacing quality.
tense exotic flamenco
Locrian
Mode VII · H W W H W W W
Unstable, dissonant, eerie. The only mode with a diminished tonic — rarely used melodically.

Non-diatonic scales
Harmonic minor
Natural minor + raised 7th
Dramatic, classical, Middle-Eastern. The raised 7th creates a yearning, exotic tension.
dramatic yearning cinematic
Pentatonic minor
5-note · 1 ♭3 4 5 ♭7
Raw, emotive, universal. Blues, rock, and folk staple — hard to play a wrong note.
bluesy raw rock
Pentatonic major
5-note · 1 2 3 5 6
Open, folk-like, timeless. Happy and simple — country, pop, and world music all use it.
folk open timeless
Whole tone
6-note · all whole steps
Ambiguous, floating, impressionistic. No sense of resolution — used for dreamlike passages.
ambiguous floating impressionist

W = whole step (2 semitones) · H = half step (1 semitone). The seven church modes all share the same parent major scale — just with a different starting degree.

Core melody concepts
Contour
shape in pitch
Foundation
The arc your melody draws in space.
Mix stepwise motion (adjacent notes) with occasional leaps for drama. Too many leaps = chaotic. All steps = boring. Leaps should be followed by resolution in the opposite direction.
Rising arch · falling line · valley shape · zigzag
shapedirection
Tension & release
stability
Tension
The heartbeat of any melody.
Move away from stable notes (1, 3, 5) toward unstable ones, then resolve back. The 7th degree is the classic pull tone — it leans hard toward the tonic. Build tension, then pay it off.
Scale degree 7 → 1 · leading tone resolution
instabilityresolution
Phrase structure
breath & form
Structure
Give the listener a sense of breath.
Think in 4 or 8-bar units. A question phrase ends unresolved (on V or 2); an answer phrase lands on the tonic. This call-and-response shape appears in virtually every genre of music.
Antecedent (question) → Consequent (answer)
call & responsephrase
Repetition
with variation
Memory
What makes a melody stick.
State a motif, repeat it (at a different pitch or rhythm), then vary it — extend it, invert it, or surprise the listener by going somewhere unexpected. Too much repetition = boring. No repetition = forgettable.
State · repeat · vary · surprise
motifdevelopment
Rhythmic identity
the first thing you hum
Rhythm
Often more important than pitch.
The rhythm of a melody is usually what you recognize first. A strong rhythmic motif with a simple pitch shape often beats a complex pitch contour with flat rhythm. Lock the rhythm, then find the notes.
Beethoven's 5th: da-da-da-DUM is rhythm first

M
Melody

Develop the idea. Sequence it, transpose it, invert it, change its rhythm. Push it somewhere new.

The engine of classical harmony.
A tritone between the 3rd and 7th creates two unstable intervals that both want to resolve. Far more directional than plain V. Almost every classical cadence uses it.

Special chords — chromatic colour
V7/X
secondary dominant
Secondary
Tonicization — a key within a key.
Any chord can be temporarily treated as a local tonic by placing its own V7 before it. Creates a brief feeling of visiting another key without fully leaving home. Bach used this constantly to give every chord a sense of destination.
A7 → Dm in C major · V7/ii → ii

♭II6
Neapolitan
Neapolitan
Tragic, operatic, final-feeling.
A major chord built on the flattened 2nd degree — always in first inversion. Pre-dominant function, leads into V. Unmistakably heavy and dramatic. Beloved by Beethoven, Schubert, and Verdi.
D♭ major (1st inv.) in C minor → G7 → Cm
tragicoperatic
It+6
Italian aug. 6th
Aug. 6th
Two notes that explode outward to V.
The simplest augmented 6th chord — two notes an augmented 6th apart that resolve outward by a half step each to the dominant. Intensely directed. Used for dramatic cadential approach.
A♭–F♯ → G–G · resolves to V in C
explosivedirected
Ger+6
German aug. 6th
Aug. 6th
The shape-shifter — enharmonic pivot.
The richest augmented 6th chord. Enharmonically identical to a dominant 7th chord, allowing composers to pivot between distant keys with a single harmonic sleight of hand. Beethoven and Schubert exploited this endlessly.
Ger+6 = enharmonic V7 of another key
pivotmodulation
Modulation — moving between keys
Pivot chord
Find a chord shared by both keys, use it as a hinge, then proceed in the new key. The smoothest, most invisible technique. The listener barely notices the key has shifted.
smoothclassical
Phrase modulation
Simply jump to the new key with no preparation — just start a new phrase in the new tonic. Jarring but effective for drama, surprise, or emotional lift. Common in pop and folk.
directdramatic
Chromatic modulation
Slide by half-step into the new key using an altered chord or enharmonic pivot. Creates a sense of harmonic surprise — the floor shifts beneath you. Romantic era hallmark.
chromaticRomantic

What truly sets classical harmony apart: voice leading (each chord note is an independent melody), functional directionality (every chord has a destination), and increasing chromaticism — from Bach's order through Romantic ambiguity all the way to Wagner's tonal dissolution.

Inversions — same notes, different bass
Root position
Root in the bass
The default and most stable voicing. The chord's name note is on the bottom. Feels grounded and complete.
C / E / G
1st inversion
3rd in the bass
Lighter and less final than root position. Often used to smooth a bass line — the bass steps by step rather than leaping. Common in baroque chorales.
E / G / C
2nd inversion
5th in the bass
The most unstable inversion — the 5th in the bass creates ambiguity. Often used as a cadential ⁶₄ chord (the I⁶₄ before V in classical cadences).
G / C / E
3rd inversion
7th in the bass (7th chords only)
For 7th chords — the 7th degree in the bass. Creates strong forward motion; the bass note strongly wants to resolve downward by step.
F / G / B / D (G7 3rd inv.)

Inversions are notated with a slash: C/E means a C major chord with E in the bass. In classical figured bass notation, root position = ⁵₃, 1st inversion = ⁶₃, 2nd inversion = ⁶₄.

Deep, thunderous, imposing. The largest orchestral drum — used for cannon shots, climaxes, and physical impact. Feels as much as heard.
Cymbals
Crashing, shimmering, explosive. Clash cymbals for dramatic peaks; suspended cymbal bowed or rolled for ethereal swells and long, shimmering decays.

Rhythmic, colorful, folk-inspired. Castanets evoke Spain and flamenco; tambourine adds jingle and rhythmic drive to dance passages.

Percussive yet singing. The widest range of any standard instrument. Used as soloist, chamber partner, and orchestral colour. Bridges harmonic and rhythmic worlds.

Parallel vs relative — the key distinction
Relative major / minor
Same notes · different tonal center
C major and A minor share identical notes — no accidentals change. Switching between them means re-emphasizing a different note as "home." Effortless and smooth — nothing actually changes except focus.
same key sig.smooth
Parallel major / minor
Same root · different key signature
C major → C minor. Same tonic, completely different emotional colour. This is the dramatic shift — you keep the anchor note but change the entire harmonic world around it. Requires accidentals.

The golden rule

No matter which technique you use, a modulation is only "locked in" once the ear hears a V→I cadence in the new key. Until then, you're just visiting. If you want a brief colour without committing, avoid a cadence — drift in, drift back out.

Technique 1 — Pivot chord (smoothest)
Pivot
shared chord
Invisible
Find a chord that belongs to both keys — use it as a hinge.
The ear hears the chord as belonging to the old key, then reinterprets it as belonging to the new key. The most seamless modulation technique — a careful listener might not notice the key has changed until a cadence confirms it.
C major → G major: the IV chord (F major) becomes VII in G major · or ii (D minor) becomes vi in F major

Technique 2 — Modal mixture (most expressive)
Borrow
parallel key
Colour
Borrow one chord from the parallel minor — cast a shadow without leaving the key.
You're not fully modulating — just colouring. The iv chord (minor subdominant) borrowed into a major key is one of the most emotionally devastating moves in music. Works in reverse too: borrow IV major or VI major into a minor key for a sudden ray of light.

Technique 3 — Leading tone pivot (classical)
♯VII
raised 7th

Introduce the raised 7th of the target key — the ear immediately hears a new tonic forming.
In harmonic minor, the raised 7th is a leading tone that pulls hard toward the tonic. Inserting it as part of a V chord in the new key signals the modulation clearly. The foreign accidental acts as a signpost: something has changed.

Technique 4 — Phrase modulation (boldest)
Direct
no preparation
Dramatic
Stop the old key and simply start the new one — no pivot, no warning.
The contrast itself is the effect. Works best at structural moments — a new section, a new verse, a final chorus. The abruptness feels like an emotional jolt or a sudden shift in perspective. Many pop songs use this for a key-lifted final chorus.

Approach the new key by half-step — surprising but inevitable in retrospect.
Move to the new tonic by a semitone using an altered chord (♭VII, Neapolitan, or German aug. 6th as pivot). The floor shifts beneath the listener. Schubert, Brahms, and Wagner used chromatic slides to move between distant keys that share no diatonic pivot chords.

How extensions work

Extensions are notes stacked above the 7th — 9th, 11th, 13th. They don't change the chord's function; a dominant 9th is still a dominant chord. They add texture, sophistication, and emotional nuance. In practice you pick the colours you want, often omitting the 5th (redundant) or the natural 11th (clashes with the major 3rd).

Quick reference — extensions at a glance
Natural 6 / 9 (on I, ii)
Warm, nostalgic, lush. No tension — just richer colour. Your "make it sound better" move.
♭9 (on V)
Sinister, exotic, grinding. Dark tension amplifier before resolving to minor.
♯9 (on V)
Raw, bluesy, major+minor clash. The Hendrix chord — electric and unresolved.
♯11 (on I)
Floating, cinematic, Lydian magic. The single most useful extension for game & film music.
Natural 11 (on ii, vi)
Open, modal, spacious. Dorian ambience — good for exploratory or ambient music.
13 (on V)
Rich, full, sophisticated dominant. Jazz arrival chord — tense but mature.

Extensions are most effective when voiced carefully. Avoid the natural 11th on major chords (clashes with the 3rd). The 5th is almost always safe to omit. Stack extensions from the outside in — 13th on top, 7th closest to the root.
