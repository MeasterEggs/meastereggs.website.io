PART 01

The Foundations

1.1 — What is a Voxel?

The word voxel blends "volume" and "pixel." A pixel is a tiny square in a 2D image. A voxel is its 3D equivalent — a tiny cube of space.

Imagine filling a large glass box with tiny Lego bricks. Each brick is one voxel. When a brick is present, that spot is solid rock. When it is absent, that spot is empty air. Your entire cave world is described by which bricks are there and which are not.

Key Idea

A voxel grid is a 3D array — a list of lists of lists, one entry per point in space. For a cave game, each entry stores a floating-point number describing how solid that point is.

1.2 — Density Values

Rather than storing a simple yes/no per voxel, we store a floating-point number called a density value. It represents how deeply inside solid rock that point sits.

  • Large positive (+2.0) — deep inside solid rock
  • Near zero (±0.1) — right at the surface boundary
  • Negative (−1.0) — inside open air

The surface of the cave wall is found wherever the density crosses zero. Because we store continuous float values, the meshing algorithm can place the wall smoothly between grid points — this is what creates curved, organic walls instead of rigid stair-steps.

1.3 — Marching Cubes

Marching Cubes is the algorithm that transforms your invisible grid of numbers into visible triangles forming the cave walls. It is the translator between your data and what appears on screen.

1
Examine every small cube
The algorithm looks at every cell formed by 8 neighbouring grid corner points — like scanning each cell of a 3D checkerboard.
2
Classify each corner
For each cube, it checks whether each of its 8 corners has a positive (solid) or negative (empty) density value.
3
Look up triangle pattern
With 8 corners that can each be solid or empty, there are 256 possible combinations. A precomputed lookup table gives the correct triangle arrangement for each one.
4
Interpolate surface position
Triangles are placed along cube edges where density crosses zero, with the exact position smoothly interpolated based on the density values on either side.
5
Output the mesh
All triangles stitched together form the final visible surface — organic, curved walls that feel carved by nature.

1.4 — Mesh and Collision

A mesh is the collection of triangles that forms a visible 3D object. Every object in a game is ultimately a mesh. Marching Cubes generates vertices (corner points in 3D space) and faces (triangles connecting those points) from your density grid.

A visible mesh tells the GPU where to draw. But to stop your player from walking through walls, the physics engine needs a separate collision shape. In Godot, after generating a mesh you also generate a StaticBody3D with a trimesh collision shape from the same data.

Important

Whenever you dig and modify the voxel grid, both the mesh and the collision shape must be rebuilt for the affected region. Voxel Tools handles this for you automatically.

1.5 — Chunks

A whole cave world is too large to rebuild all at once. Digging one small hole should not force the entire cave mesh to regenerate.

The solution is to divide the world into chunks — smaller sub-grids, for example 32×32×32 voxels each. When you dig, only the chunk or chunks around the dig site are updated. The rest remain unchanged.

Note

Voxel Tools handles chunk management automatically. You define chunk size and world bounds — it handles loading, unloading, and rebuilding as needed.

PART 02

Cave Generation Theory

2.1 — The Overall Strategy

Your cave has requirements that guide algorithm selection: one large central room, smaller satellite rooms hidden behind diggable walls, a bounded total area, elevation variation, and placeable furniture.

A single algorithm cannot satisfy all of these elegantly. Instead, a pipeline is used — each step handles one concern and feeds its results into the next.

2.2 — Binary Space Partitioning (BSP)

What it is: A method of dividing a rectangular space into smaller rectangles by making recursive cuts.

Imagine taking a sheet of paper and cutting it in half — then cutting one of those halves — and so on. Each final rectangle is a candidate location for a room.

BSP solves two problems at once: rooms never overlap, and total space used stays within your bounding box. Minimum and maximum room sizes are controlled by setting limits on when splitting stops.

In Your Cave

BSP defines the large central room and positions of smaller satellite rooms, all within the overall bounding area, before any carving begins.

2.3 — Drunkard's Walk

What it is: A pathfinding method where an agent moves step by step in a random direction, carving empty space wherever it goes.

Picture a mole that starts at the central room and wanders randomly until it reaches a satellite room. Every step it takes, it carves out a small sphere of rock. The result is a winding, organic tunnel — guaranteed to connect the rooms but never in a straight line.

  • Guarantees connectivity — there will always be a path between rooms
  • Randomness gives tunnels a natural, non-straight character
  • Run multiple walkers simultaneously for branching passages

Voxels immediately surrounding each tunnel are marked as diggable walls, letting the player expand tunnels or discover adjacent spaces.

2.4 — Cellular Automata Smoothing

What it is: A cleanup pass that makes rough, jagged spaces look more natural.

After carving with BSP and Drunkard's Walk, the edges of rooms and tunnels can look angular. Cellular Automata applies a simple rule across the grid: if a voxel is surrounded mostly by empty space, it becomes empty too. If surrounded mostly by rock, it stays solid.

Running this rule three to five times smooths walls and ceilings, removes isolated floating bits of rock, and softens sharp corners. The result feels carved by nature rather than by a computer.

2.5 — Elevation and Layering

Elevation is handled by treating the 3D grid honestly — rooms and tunnels can be placed at different Y positions. Several techniques add believable height variation:

  • The central room spans multiple vertical layers with a high ceiling
  • Tunnels slope upward or downward between rooms at different heights
  • Floor density values are nudged slightly to create subtle inclines
  • Larger carved spheres at tunnel endpoints create natural chamber transitions
PART 03

Practical Implementation in Godot 4

3.1 — The VoxelGrid Class

This is the core data structure. Everything else reads from or writes to it. It stores a one-dimensional array of floats, treated as a 3D grid using index arithmetic.

GDScript — VoxelGrid skeleton
class_name VoxelGrid extends RefCounted var size_x: int var size_y: int var size_z: int var data: PackedFloat32Array func get_index(x, y, z) -> int: return x + size_x * (y + size_y * z) func carve_sphere(cx, cy, cz, radius): # Write negative density with smooth falloff # density = distance_from_centre - radius
Off-by-one warning

With Marching Cubes, grid points are corners, not cells. A 32×32×32 chunk requires 33×33×33 grid points. Always allocate one extra point per axis.

3.2 — The Cave Generator

This class is pure logic — it reads nothing from Godot and produces nothing visual. It writes density values into the VoxelGrid. The pipeline runs in this order:

1
Define bounding box
Set overall grid size based on your desired surface area range.
2
BSP room placement
Recursively split the bounding box until you have one large region and several smaller ones.
3
Carve rooms
Call carve_sphere() for each room position and size to write negative density.
4
Drunkard's Walk tunnels
For each satellite room, run a walker from the central room toward it, carving as it moves.
5
Mark diggable walls
Every voxel within 1–2 units of a tunnel gets flagged as DIGGABLE.
6
Cellular Automata pass
Smooth the grid by running the neighbour rule 3–5 times.
7
Elevation adjustments
Shift room Y positions and slope tunnel paths for height variation.

3.3 — Voxel Tools by Zylann

Rather than implementing Marching Cubes yourself — a complex algorithm with many edge cases — use the open-source Voxel Tools plugin for Godot 4 by developer Zylann.

  • A VoxelTerrain node that manages the world automatically
  • Automatic Marching Cubes meshing of your density grid
  • Chunk management — only rebuilds what changes
  • Collision generation from the mesh
  • A VoxelTool API for reading and writing at runtime (for digging)
Where to find it

Install from the Godot Asset Library or search godot_voxel by Zylann on GitHub. You write the cave generator; Voxel Tools handles everything between your data and the visible, collidable cave.

3.4 — The FPS Controller

Godot 4's CharacterBody3D node is the foundation for your player. The three components you need:

  • CharacterBody3D — the physics body that interacts with collision
  • Camera3D — the first-person view, placed at head height inside the body
  • CollisionShape3D — a capsule shape representing the player's physical volume

Godot's documentation includes a ready-made FPS demo project. Start with that so you can focus on cave generation rather than movement code.

3.5 — The Dig System

Digging is what makes the cave feel alive and responsive. The loop each frame:

1
Cast a ray
Godot's RayCast3D node casts forward from the centre of the camera every frame.
2
Check voxel type
When the ray hits a surface, look up whether that voxel is marked DIGGABLE.
3
Write negative density
On dig input, call VoxelTool.set_voxel_f() with a negative value at the hit position and surrounding points.
4
Voxel Tools rebuilds
The plugin detects the change and automatically rebuilds the mesh and collision for the affected chunk.

A dig radius of 1.5 to 2.0 voxel units is a good starting point.

3.6 — Object and Furniture Placement

After generation, a placement pass scans the grid for valid spawn positions. A position is valid when all four conditions are met:

  • The voxel at that position is empty (air)
  • The voxel directly below is solid (floor)
  • There is enough vertical clearance above (at least 2 units for most objects)
  • The position is not already occupied

Each room type has its own weighted list of furniture. Godot's RandomNumberGenerator handles weighted selection. Objects are placed as standard Godot Node3D scenes instantiated at valid positions — no voxel system interaction required.

PART 04

Textures and Visuals

4.1 — The UV Problem

UV coordinates are the 2D texture coordinates assigned to each vertex. They tell the GPU which part of a texture image to draw at each surface point. With Marching Cubes, triangles are generated dynamically in arbitrary orientations — there is no natural way to assign UV coordinates.

The solution is triplanar mapping. Instead of UVs, the shader reads the texture three times — once along each axis (X, Y, Z) — and blends the results based on the direction the surface faces.

Good News

Godot's StandardMaterial3D has a built-in triplanar option. Enable uv1_triplanar = true and your textures tile correctly across any Marching Cubes surface — no shader code needed.

4.2 — Recommended Visual Progression

Visuals can be added incrementally at any point. Build the cave logic first, then layer in polish.

5.3 — Glossary

Voxel
A unit of 3D space, like a 3D pixel. The basic building block of the cave world.
Density value
A floating-point number at each grid point. Positive means solid, negative means empty, zero is the surface.
Iso-value
The threshold at which the surface is drawn. Usually zero. Adjusting it makes caves feel tighter or more open.
Marching Cubes
The algorithm that converts density values into visible triangles by examining every cube of 8 neighbouring grid points.
Mesh
A collection of triangles (faces) and their corner points (vertices) that forms a visible 3D surface.
Collision shape
The invisible geometry used by the physics engine to detect contact between objects.
Chunk
A sub-region of the world grid that is meshed and managed independently for performance.
BSP
Binary Space Partitioning — a method of dividing space into non-overlapping rectangles recursively.
Drunkard's Walk
A tunnel-carving method where an agent moves randomly toward a target, carving as it goes.
Cellular Automata
A smoothing pass where each voxel's state is updated based on its neighbours, making cave surfaces look natural.
Triplanar mapping
A texturing technique that projects textures from all three axes simultaneously, solving the UV problem for Marching Cubes.
Normal map
A texture that adds the appearance of surface bumps and detail without adding extra triangles to the mesh.
PBR
Physically Based Rendering — a modern approach to materials that simulates how light interacts with real-world surfaces.
